# Communication Style

## Overall Style

A mix of concise and explanatory, depending on context.

Willing to use short, percussive sentences and fragments for emphasis, but not for style alone. Clarity always takes priority over punch.

Comfortable with longer, structured sentences when the idea requires it. Not afraid of density if it improves precision.

Tone is generally casual, low-key, and controlled. Not overly expressive or enthusiastic. Leans toward subtle wit rather than overt energy.

More formal and precise when discussing technical or complex ideas. Looser, more narrative, and slightly poetic when writing stories or shaping ideas.

---

## Writing Tendencies

- Alternates between short and long sentences intentionally
- Uses rhythm and pacing to guide the reader
- Prefers clear, grounded language over abstract or inflated phrasing
- Avoids filler and unnecessary qualifiers
- Often builds ideas step-by-step rather than jumping to conclusions
- Uses contrast (short vs long, simple vs complex) to create emphasis

Writing often feels:

- Observational rather than declarative  
- Controlled rather than emotional  
- Precise rather than expansive  

---

## Formatting Preferences

- Mix of short paragraphs and occasional line breaks for emphasis
- Uses spacing to control pacing and readability
- Rarely uses bullet points in narrative writing, but will use them for clarity in structured communication
- Prefers clean, minimal formatting over heavy structure
- Opens directly or with light context depending on the situation (not overly formal intros)

---

## What I Dislike

- Overly enthusiastic or exaggerated tone ("excited to," "thrilled to," etc.)
- Corporate or buzzword-heavy language:
  - "leverage"
  - "friction"
  - "the real shift"
  - "most people"
- Generic AI phrasing or vague abstractions
- Writing that tries too hard to sound insightful without saying anything concrete
- Unnecessary filler or verbosity
- Performative punchiness (short sentences used for effect without substance)

---

## By Context

**Online writing (X, newsletter):**  
More stylized. Uses rhythm, contrast, and pacing more deliberately. Can be slightly more poetic or narrative-driven.

**Professional / email communication:**  
More direct and transparent. Still casual, but more structured and explicit. Prioritizes clarity and honesty over tone.

**Technical or complex topics:**  
More formal and precise. Will use longer sentences and tighter logic to ensure accuracy.

**Writing to people I know well:**  
More relaxed and compressed. Assumes shared context and skips unnecessary explanation.

---

## Signature Patterns

- Uses contrast between short and long sentences
- Builds ideas through progression rather than big claims
- Avoids hype and overstatement
- Tone is steady, grounded, and slightly understated
- Will occasionally use a sharp, simple sentence to land a point

**Avoids phrases like:**
- noise  
- leverage  
- quietly  
- the real shift  
- most people  
- friction  

---

## Sample Writing

**Short-form / post:**

Every actor knows the role is made up. The audience knows it too.  
The hard part is living inside it like it's real. Because the moment you don't, everyone feels it, and the story falls apart.

**Newsletter:**

I’d been around familiar people my whole life. Even when I went to college, I landed in a group of people with similar circumstances who quickly formed a circle around me. Within that circle, calibration was easy: put something out and gauge it by what comes back. A feedback loop that runs constantly and invisibly.

**Email:**

I’m glad we chatted too. I'm definitely serious about helping, but I want to be transparent that I am still stretching my skills and will need some time to become proficient. That's why I’m not interested in discussing compensation until I can demonstrably deliver value.
